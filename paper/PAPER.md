# When the Benchmark Grades Itself: Two Information Leaks in Football Player-Representation Evaluation

*Draft (June 2026). Numbers from local reproducible runs; related-work citations verified against sources. Future-work (not blockers): RisingBALLER reproduction; a fully-independent second dataset; 6MapNet's trained triplet-CNN under a cross-match split (needs tracking data). (The *static heatmap family* cross-match collapse is now tested directly — see Sec. 4.)*

## Abstract
Learned representations of football (soccer) players are routinely reported to capture playing style, yet the field validates them in ways that **leak the relevance signal into the model being graded**. We identify two such leaks, give leakage-free protocols and corrected numbers, and release an open external benchmark. **Feature leakage (P2):** a set-transformer over anonymized StatsBomb 360 freeze-frames scores 0.878 within-tournament player-identity Top-1 under random split-half but **collapses to 0.188 under a match-disjoint (cross-match) split** (disjoint 95% CIs); a same-match positive control scores 0.889 — within-match information alone is near-sufficient — implicating match-context recognition rather than enduring style. The collapse reproduces for *static heatmap representations* (a parameter-free multi-channel heatmap, 0.806→0.113; a Player-Vectors-style NMF, 0.595→0.123), so it is a property of the random-within-tournament split protocol rather than one architecture; we do **not** claim a discriminatively-trained CNN such as 6MapNet collapses identically (untested — it needs tracking data). **Label leakage (P1):** graded against its own clusters/archetypes a model reaches nDCG@10 ≈ 0.99; on an *external, transfer-anchored* benchmark (ScoutBench, graded against realized like-for-like replacement signings the model never saw), every method beats random, but **no learned representation we test beats a raw-stat kNN on all-candidates retrieval (the primary metric) — a null the benchmark is powered to detect a ≥0.007-MAP advantage against** (we report the measured sensitivity). That holds for an off-objective contrastive embedding, a **faithful reproduction of Player Vectors** (Decroos & Davis), an NMF baseline, a **purpose-trained, player-disjoint metric-learner** (no improvement across 3 seeds; partial equivalence under a ±0.005 TOST), and representations built from genuinely **orthogonal richer inputs** (event-transformer sequences, VAEP, 360 spatial features). Within-role (same-position) retrieval is near-floor and *underpowered* — the conservative test's minimum detectable effect (≈0.017 MAP) exceeds the entire best-vs-random spread — so we draw conclusions only on all-candidates. A methodological corollary: the field's default per-query significance test treats ~1363 dependent queries (they cluster into ~22 position components, effective count ≈ 8) as independent, **understating variance** (the naive p is ~10× too small). In the spirit of *A Metric Learning Reality Check* (Musgrave et al., ECCV 2020) and the tabular-DL-vs-GBDT literature.

## 1. Introduction
Player-similarity search ("find a midfielder like X"), style fingerprinting, and representation learning over event/spatial data are active in football analytics. But how are these representations *evaluated*? We find two pervasive pitfalls:
- **P1 — circular relevance.** Similarity quality is judged against labels derived from the model itself (k-means archetypes, role clusters) or anecdotes. A model graded on its own partition can score near-perfectly (our legacy text bench: nDCG@10 ≈ 0.99) while failing any external test.
- **P2 — match-context leakage.** Identity/consistency is scored by splitting a player's events at random within a tournament, so events from the *same match* land in both query and gallery. The model can then recognize "same game" rather than enduring style.
We instantiate external/leakage-free protocols for each, and report what learned representations actually achieve.

## 2. Related work
- **Survey anchor:** Davis et al., *Methodology and Evaluation in Sports Analytics: Challenges, Approaches, and Lessons Learned* (Machine Learning, Springer, 2024) — catalogs the consecutive-season nearest-neighbor protocol and broader evaluation pitfalls; supports our framing that player-similarity/representation is validated circularly or qualitatively.
- **Player Vectors** (Decroos & Davis, ECML-PKDD 2019) — per-type NMF over action-location heatmaps; evaluated via consecutive-season *player-identity* retrieval (objective same-player labels) + qualitative scouting case studies — *not* external transfer ground truth.
- **RisingBALLER** (Adjileye, arXiv 2410.00943, StatsBomb Conf 2024) — players-as-tokens transformer FM; its quantitative task is next-match-statistics prediction; "similar player" is qualitative only. (Reproducing it on ScoutBench is future-work — a transformer FM at training scale.)
- **SoccerMix** (Decroos, Van Roy & Davis, ECML-PKDD 2020) — soft-clustering mixture model for action/style representation, evaluated qualitatively. **Football2Vec** (Magdaci, 2021; *Towards Data Science* series + `github.com/ofirmg/football2vec`, not peer-reviewed) — 32-dim Word2Vec-style player embeddings, qualitative cosine-NN. Both rely on self-derived / qualitative evaluation.
- **Spatial similarity index** (Gómez-Rubio, Lagos & Palmí-Perales, arXiv 2412.08303, 2024) — a heatmap-based spatial-statistics index (Lee's statistic / Moran's I over kernel-smoothed position rasters, La Liga via Wyscout) for scouting, **demonstrated only through illustrative case studies, with no external relevance ground truth and no released benchmark**. A recent instance of the qualitative/self-validated similarity evaluation ScoutBench is built to replace; its heatmap representation is also in the static-heatmap family (Sec. 4), though it does similarity rather than within-match identity, so we do not treat it as a P2 example.
- Method genre: **A Metric Learning Reality Check** (Musgrave, Belongie & Lim, ECCV 2020); tabular DL < GBDT (Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, *Information Fusion* 2022; Grinsztajn et al., NeurIPS 2022 Datasets & Benchmarks); dimensional collapse in contrastive learning (Jing, Vincent, LeCun & Tian, ICLR 2022) as a mechanism for "lossy" projections.
- External-outcome evaluation that exists but is distinct: **market-value prediction** (e.g., explainable-ML valuation, arXiv 2311.04599 — predicts a scalar *value*, not similarity) and **transfer-impact forecasting** (Transfer Portal; Dinsdale & Gallagher, arXiv 2201.11533 — a forecasting target). Neither uses realized replacements as a *similarity-retrieval* ground truth — the precise gap ScoutBench fills.
- **6MapNet** (Kim et al., arXiv 2109.04720, MLSA@ECML-PKDD 2021) — triplet network for player identity; the closest neighbor to our 360-identity task. It splits by sampling player *phases* per entity for val/test rather than by match, so phases from one match can fall in both query and gallery — it may be **vulnerable to the same match-context leakage** we study, a candidate to re-evaluate under a match-disjoint split (a tractable follow-up), not a safe reference.
- (We deliberately do *not* cite Shaw & Gopaladesikan "Routine Inspection" here — it is a corner-kick paper, unrelated to transfer-similarity.)

## 2.5 A unifying view: evaluation leakage
Both pitfalls are one failure — the evaluation's **independence assumption between the relevance signal and the model under test is violated**:
- **Label leakage (P1):** relevance labels are produced *by the model* (its own clusters/archetypes). The model is graded against its own output → near-perfect, meaningless scores.
- **Feature leakage (P2):** query and gallery share *input features from the same match*, so the model can match shared context instead of the target property (enduring identity/style).

| leakage source ↓ / task → | Similarity retrieval | Identity / consistency |
|---|---|---|
| **Label** leakage | self-derived archetype relevance → nDCG≈0.99 (**fix: external transfer anchor — ScoutBench**) | n/a (identity labels are objective) |
| **Feature** leakage | mitigated (candidates are *other* players) | within-tournament split-half → 0.88 (**fix: match-disjoint split**) |

These are two *distinct* contamination mechanisms, not one dressed up twice: P1 violates **label–model independence** (the relevance labels are generated by the model under test), while P2 violates **query–gallery independence** (query and gallery share nuisance match-context beyond the target property — the identity label itself is objective and independent of the model). Valid retrieval evaluation requires *both* conditions; each pitfall breaks a different one, and each fix restores the broken one — an external label anchor for P1, a match-disjoint split for P2. The shared lesson is not a single mechanism but a discipline: identify every path from the relevance signal to the representation and sever it.

## 3. Pitfall 1 — circular relevance, and ScoutBench
**ScoutBench Task B.** Ground truth = realized like-for-like replacement transfers (CC0 transfermarkt): a club loses player X at sub-position P and signs Y at P in the same/next window ⇒ (X,Y) is a silver similarity-positive. Each query player is ranked against all others by embedding cosine; we report MAP/Hit@k/MRR over all candidates (**primary**) and same-sub-position candidates (stratified). n=1363 query players from 5868 pairs; gallery 5668 players (StatsBomb-open-derived; research-only).

**Statistics done right.** The query→relevant graph collapses into **22 connected components** (≈ sub-positions), so queries are NOT independent; we use a **block bootstrap resampling components** (B=10000) and report a conservative **cluster-level paired t** over the ~22 component means, with **Holm** correction across the comparison family. Per-query bootstrap (which we show is wrong) treats ~1363 dependent queries as independent — understating variance ~1.3x and making the naive p ~10x too small (i.e., inflating significance). We also note the block bootstrap (query-weighted) and the component-t (component-equal-weighted) estimate *different* targets — effective component count is ≈8 — so we report both rather than calling one a "conservative" version of the other.

**Results.** **All-candidates MAP (ALL-MAP) is the primary metric**; same-position MAP (SP-MAP) is the stratified within-role test, which we show below is near-floor and *underpowered*. The table is sorted by SP-MAP for continuity with prior work; read ALL-MAP for the conclusions.
| method | SP-MAP | ALL-MAP |
|---|---|---|
| fbref percentile | 0.0514 | 0.0082 |
| pca(card) | 0.0501 | 0.0195 |
| raw_card cosine | 0.0498 | 0.0194 |
| TF-IDF text | 0.0469 | 0.0165 |
| **Player-Vectors (faithful, Decroos&Davis heatmap-NMF)** | 0.0464 | 0.0172 |
| NMF (over card features) | 0.0450 | 0.0135 |
| **v11 (contrastive embed)** | 0.0447 | 0.0162 |
| card+VAEP | 0.0440 | 0.0088 |
| random | 0.0369 | 0.0030 |

- **Signal exists (on all-candidates):** raw_card vs random ALL-MAP +0.0163 is significant under both the block bootstrap (p=0.0002) and the conservative component-t (p=0.0001). On *same-position* the signal is weaker — significant under the block bootstrap (p=0.0002) but **not** the component-t (p=0.15) — consistent with the position prior dominating within-role (a random vector already scores SP-Hit@10 ≈ 0.18). The robust signal-exists claim is therefore all-candidates; the same-position regime is near-floor.
- **Benchmark sensitivity — the null is well-powered on the primary metric, near-floor on the stratified one.** We measure the minimum detectable effect (MDE) of the conservative component-t directly from the per-component variance (power 0.8, α=0.05; `scoutbench_power.py`). **All-candidates (primary): MDE ≈ 0.007 MAP, below the best-vs-random spread (0.0163)** — the test resolves spread-sized effects: it detects raw≫random (component diff +0.0097, p≈0) and would detect a ≥0.007-MAP learned-rep advantage, yet the learned reps sit *at or below* raw (raw−v11 +0.0008, p=0.49; raw−Player-Vectors −0.0027, p=0.47). So "no learned representation beats raw on all-candidates" is a **genuine well-powered null**, not test insensitivity. **Same-position: MDE ≈ 0.017 MAP, exceeding the entire 0.0145 spread** — the within-role test cannot even resolve raw≫random (p=0.15), because the position prior dominates (a random vector already scores SP-Hit@10 ≈ 0.18). We therefore **draw no conclusions from same-position differences** (including the apparent raw–v11 +0.005 gap); they are below the label's resolution. This is itself the point: circular self-labels manufacture an effect (nDCG≈0.99) far above this floor, which is why they look near-perfect.
- **Off-objective embedding does not beat raw:** on **all-candidates** (primary, well-powered) v11 sits at-or-below raw (raw−v11 +0.0008, component-t p=0.49) — a powered null. The nominal raw−v11 +0.0051 *same-position* gap is significant only under the query-weighted block bootstrap (p≈0.02), does **not** survive Holm, and is **not robust to the component-t (p=0.36)**; given the same-position MDE (≈0.017) it is below the benchmark's resolution and we do not rest any claim on it.
- **The faithful, published Player-Vectors baseline also does not beat raw (the key external-baseline check):** a faithful reproduction of Player Vectors (Decroos & Davis, ECML-PKDD 2019) — 50×50 action-location heatmaps per type (pass start+end, shot/cross/dribble start) → per-type NMF, built from on-ball events, 100% gallery coverage (5668 players), *not* our card features — scores SP-MAP 0.0464 / ALL-MAP 0.0172. It is **not significantly different from raw_card** (raw−PV: SP +0.0034 block p=0.15, component-t p=0.90; ALL +0.0022 p=0.15). So the canonical published learned representation ties/slightly-trails a raw-stat kNN. This moves the claim from "our models lose" to "learned representations, **including the published Player-Vectors method**, do not beat raw." (A secondary NMF over the card features behaves the same: SP-MAP 0.0450, below raw on the query-weighted estimand p=0.003, but not robust to component-level multiplicity correction.)
- **Purpose-trained, player-disjoint metric learning does not significantly beat raw (the decisive, non-tautological test — multi-positive loss bug fixed):** a projection trained directly on the replacement objective, with a **multi-positive InfoNCE** masking each player's *other* true replacements (the graph is many-to-many; a naive one-positive-per-row loss creates contradictory labels), evaluated on player-disjoint held-out queries across **3 seeds**: same-position ΔMAP (metric − raw) = {+0.0008, +0.0006, −0.0027}, all p>0.37; all-candidates ΔMAP = {+0.0016, +0.0024, +0.0009}, consistently small-positive but never significant (p 0.19–0.68). A predeclared **TOST equivalence test (±0.005 MAP)** concludes statistical equivalence to raw in 1 of 3 seeds and is **inconclusive** in the other two (95% CIs marginally exceed the margin, e.g. [−0.006, +0.007]). So the honest statement is: **no improvement over raw in any seed, and equivalence only partially established** — a metric learned directly on these features does not beat raw cosine, though we cannot fully rule out a sub-0.005-MAP gain. [Future: tighter CIs via more seeds / larger test sets.]
- **Richer, *orthogonal* inputs also do not beat raw on the primary metric (the strongest test):** every rep above only *reshapes* the 88-d card. We therefore test inputs the card does not contain — a 256-d event-transformer embedding over SPADL event *sequences* (100% coverage; similarity-structure Pearson r ≈ 0.01 vs the card, i.e. genuinely orthogonal), 3-d VAEP, and a StatsBomb-360 spatial fingerprint (923-player tournament subset). Fused with the card via training-free cosine kNN, **none beats raw_card on all-candidates** (card+event SP 0.0503 vs 0.0498; card+VAEP and card+event+VAEP ≤ raw; card+360, on its subset, 0.0936 vs raw 0.1007), and a metric-MLP over the fused inputs (player-disjoint, 3 seeds) likewise does not (SP ΔMAP +0.004 ± 0.004, all seeds ns). **The one positive lead, reported on the same footing as every other claim, is the event embedding *alone* within-position:** SP-MAP **0.0703 vs raw 0.0498** (block p=0.0002) — the largest within-role effect we observe. We do not count it as a win, but for *exactly the reason the within-role regime decides nothing*: it fails the component-t (p=0.11) and does not transfer to all-candidates (0.0093 vs 0.0194) — held to the identical near-floor/underpowered standard (SP MDE ≈ 0.017) we apply to raw-vs-random and raw-vs-v11, not a stricter one. So event sequences are the **most promising direction for within-role discrimination and the obvious next experiment**, not noise to be dismissed — they simply do not clear the bar on the metric the benchmark can adjudicate (all-candidates). Code: `scoutbench_richfeatures.py`.
- **The circular alternative:** the same v11 scores nDCG@10 ≈ 0.99 on a bench whose relevance is its own k-means labels. External grading reverses the verdict.

**Honest scope of the negative result:** absolute numbers are low (all methods retrieve the realized replacement rarely; Hit@10 ≈ 0.27 same-position, and a random vector already gets 0.175 there because the position prior dominates). The contribution is the **ordering and the external protocol**, not deployable accuracy; the safe claim is *"no learned representation we test beats a raw-stat kNN on all-candidates external transfer-anchored similarity"* — a null the benchmark is **powered** to overturn (MDE ≈ 0.007 < the method spread), not a large-margin positive result. The faithful Player-Vectors reproduction (above) makes this a claim about a *published* learned method, not only our own models. We deliberately confine the claim to all-candidates: same-position is near-floor and underpowered (above). [Remaining baseline work: RisingBALLER (a transformer foundation model — days of training, genuine future-work) and 6MapNet's exact triplet-CNN under a cross-match split (needs tracking data; we test the static heatmap family in Sec. 4).]

**Generalization across player pools.** We re-run Task B independently on two disjoint pools split by the league the card was built from: **A** = top-5 men's club leagues (3049 players, 756 queries) and **B** = international tournaments + women's + ISL/other (2619 players, 479 queries). The conclusion replicates in both: no learned representation beats raw (Pool A: raw 0.0639 vs v11 0.0637 ns, vs Player-Vectors 0.0598 ns; Pool B: raw 0.0841 vs v11 0.0648, vs PV 0.0677, neither learned method ahead), and raw beats random in both (Pool A +0.0182, block p<0.001 *and* component-t p=0.003 — the cleanest signal-exists result; Pool B +0.0222, block p=0.007). So the finding is not an artifact of the specific pool. (Absolute MAPs are higher within each homogeneous pool — replacement retrieval is easier among more similar candidates.) Code: `scoutbench_generalization.py`.

## 4. Pitfall 2 — within-tournament match-context leakage (360 identity)
**Task.** Player-identity retrieval from anonymized 360 freeze-frames (per on-ball event: up to 23 player point-tokens x,y,actor/teammate/opponent). A set-transformer is trained contrastively; evaluated on **held-out players** (701 train / 350 test; 528,059 events; WC2022+Euro2024+Euro2020, 166 matches). Identical model across conditions; **only the query/target split changes.**

| condition | within-tournament Top-1 [95% CI] | MRR |
|---|---|---|
| split-half (random, standard) | **0.878** [0.846, 0.905] | 0.924 |
| same-match control (disjoint halves of ONE match) | **0.889** [0.859, 0.917] | 0.932 |
| **cross-match (disjoint matches — leakage-free)** | **0.188** [0.153, 0.224] | 0.326 |
| cross-match, no teammate tokens | 0.137 [0.107, 0.171] | 0.274 |
| reference: cross-tournament / random | 0.178 / 0.007 | — |

95% CIs are player-level bootstrap (resampling held-out players). The split-half and cross-match intervals are **disjoint**, so the collapse is unambiguous. It is **not a single-pool artifact**: the macro-average (each of the 3 tournaments weighted equally) matches the pooled number (split-half 0.88, cross-match 0.19), and every tournament collapses (cross-match per-tournament Top-1 = 0.24 / 0.15 / 0.19 vs split-half 0.95 / 0.86 / 0.83).

- **Collapse:** split-half 0.878 → cross-match 0.188 (Δ0.69), at **comparable event volume** (~137 vs ~141 events/side — not a data artifact).
- **Within-match information is near-sufficient:** the same-match positive control scores 0.889 — when query and target share a match, identity is nearly perfect; when they do not, it collapses. *Caveat:* the control halves a single match (~71 events/side), so it is **not** volume-matched to split-half/cross-match (~140/side); it isolates the *presence* of match-shared information, not a volume-controlled effect. Defensible conclusion: **random within-profile splitting substantially exploits match-specific information** — we cannot cleanly separate match-ID from team/lineup/role-within-that-match from genuine short-term individual behavior.
- **A small leakage-free signal survives:** cross-match 0.188 ≫ random 0.007 (dropping to 0.137 without teammate tokens). Some individual/structural signal persists, but its interpretation as *individual style* is bounded by the confounds named below.
- **The collapse is not architecture-specific — with a bounded generalization claim.** *Static* heatmap representations collapse the same way under a match-disjoint split: a parameter-free multi-channel heatmap (actor, teammate-density, opponent-density) drops from 0.806 [0.768, 0.838] split-half to 0.113 [0.083, 0.146] cross-match; a Player-Vectors-style NMF reduction drops from 0.595 [0.549, 0.639] to 0.123 [0.093, 0.155]. Both have disjoint split-half/cross-match CIs and a high same-match control (0.77 and 0.49). So the leak is a property of the within-tournament random-split *protocol*, not of any single architecture — it strikes a parameter-free histogram and an NMF reduction exactly as it strikes our trained set-transformer. **Scope caveat (important):** these are *static, untrained* representations. We do **not** claim a discriminatively-trained triplet CNN such as 6MapNet collapses identically — a learned model could in principle acquire match-invariant features a static histogram cannot. What we show is that the protocol leaks for the *heatmap input family* on which published methods rest (Player Vectors directly; 6MapNet's heatmap inputs); 6MapNet's exact triplet-CNN needs tracking data we lack and remains untested. Code: `sb360_6mapnet_crossmatch.py`.
- **Implication (stated carefully — we do NOT accuse the literature):** within-tournament *random* split-half is a tempting, seemingly-natural protocol that **would** inflate identity ~4.7–6× via match-context leakage. But the established literature does not obviously use random within-tournament split-half: Player Vectors (Decroos & Davis) evaluates *consecutive-season* consistency — match-disjoint w.r.t. exact same-match overlap (though team/role context can still recur across seasons). 6MapNet, the closest neighbor, samples player *phases* (potentially several from one match) into train/val/test rather than splitting by match — and the static heatmap *input family* it builds on collapses under our match-disjoint split (above), so a phase-sampling protocol like 6MapNet's is exposed *in principle*. Whether its trained triplet-CNN actually collapses is **untested** (it needs tracking data we lack); we flag it as a candidate to re-evaluate, **not** a proven case and **not** a safe reference. Our result is therefore a **cautionary protocol finding plus a cheaper safe alternative**: *cross-match-within-season* splitting recovers match-disjointness without requiring multiple seasons, and a same-match positive control should accompany any identity claim.
- **Residual threats we name (none fatal):** (1) *Train-time* leakage — the encoder was trained with random-half (same-match) positives, so it may have *learned* to exploit match context; a model trained with cross-match positives might recover signal. We therefore claim only that, *as trained and evaluated*, the 0.88 is match-context — NOT that individual style is unlearnable from 360. (2) The no-teammate ablation (0.137) also removes ~half the input tokens, confounding capacity with leakage type; a cleaner test permutes teammate identities rather than deleting tokens (future work).

## 5. Contribution: leakage-free protocols
1. **ScoutBench Task B** — external transfer-anchored similarity retrieval (open evaluator, block-bootstrap + cluster-t + Holm, all-candidates primary).
2. **Cross-match identity** — match-disjoint query/target (volume-comparable to split-half, ~140 events/side) plus a same-match positive control.
Both ship with code and corrected baselines; both are cheap to adopt.

## 6. Recommendations
- Never grade similarity against the model's own labels; use external/outcome anchors and report the random-baseline floor.
- For identity/consistency, split by match (or season), never randomly within a competition; include a same-match control.
- Treat queries as clustered (position components); use cluster-level inference; report absolute floors, effect sizes, and the test's **minimum detectable effect** — a null is only informative if the benchmark could have detected a real advantage of the relevant size (here, well-powered on all-candidates, near-floor within-role).

## 7. Threats to validity / limitations
- Silver labels: realized replacements encode budget/availability/position need, not pure similarity. **Validity check (done):** realized replacements sit at mean similarity-percentile **0.549** among same-position players (sub-position-clustered CI [0.521, 0.585], excludes the 0.50 null; replacement cosine 0.449 vs random-same-position 0.404). So the label carries genuine but WEAK stylistic similarity signal beyond position — not pure noise, but noisy. This coheres the benchmark: weak label signal ⇒ all methods beat random yet cluster near the floor and none separates cleanly (the raw card captures the linearly-available similarity; the residual is mostly non-stylistic transfer noise). Code: `scoutbench_label_validity.py`. [Optional further proxy: same-player-across-seasons consistency.]
- Single dataset family (StatsBomb open + Wyscout public); non-commercial. Cross-*pool* generalization is shown (the result replicates on top-5-men's-leagues and on tournaments/women's/other — Sec. 3), but cross-*dataset* generalization (a fully independent provider) remains future-work.
- ScoutBench learned-model coverage: a faithful published Player-Vectors baseline (ties raw), v11, a purpose-trained metric-learner, NMF, card+VAEP — all fail to beat raw. RisingBALLER (transformer FM) remains future-work.
- 360 follow-up: training WITH cross-match positives might learn a less-leaky encoder and partially recover signal — an open question, not a refutation.
